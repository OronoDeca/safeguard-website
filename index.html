Common Scams Targeting Seniors

Knowledge is your best defense. Learn to recognize these scams.

The Grandparent Scam

A scammer calls pretending to be your grandchild in trouble, claiming they need money urgently for bail, medical bills, or to fix a car accident. They'll beg you not to tell their parents.

Red Flag: They ask you to send money immediately via wire transfer or gift cards. Always verify by calling your grandchild directly at their known number.

Government Impersonation

Scammers pose as IRS agents, Social Security Administration, or Medicare officials claiming there's a problem with your benefits, taxes, or that your Social Security number has been suspended.

Red Flag: Real government agencies send letters first and NEVER demand immediate payment or threaten arrest over the phone.

Tech Support Scam

You receive a pop-up warning or phone call claiming your computer has a virus. The "technician" asks for remote access to your computer and payment to fix the fake problem.

Red Flag: Legitimate tech companies don't cold-call customers. Never give remote access to your computer to unsolicited callers.

Fake Charity Scam

After natural disasters or around holidays, scammers create fake charities or impersonate real ones to collect donations that never reach those in need.

Red Flag: High-pressure tactics and requests for cash, gift cards, or wire transfers. Always research charities independently before donating.

Lottery & Sweepstakes Scam

You're told you've won a prize or lottery but need to pay taxes or fees upfront to claim your winnings.

Red Flag: You can't win a contest you didn't enter. Legitimate prizes never require upfront payment.

Romance Scam

Someone you meet online develops a quick romantic relationship with you, then creates an emergency situation requiring money. They may claim to be overseas or unable to meet in person.

Red Flag: They profess love quickly, avoid video calls, and always have an excuse why they can't meet. Never send money to someone you've only met online.

How to Protect Yourself

Simple steps to keep your money and information safe

1

Slow Down

Scammers create urgency. Take your time. Legitimate organizations will give you time to think and verify. Never make financial decisions under pressure.

2

Verify Independently

Don't use contact information provided by the caller. Look up official numbers yourself and call back. Ask questions only the real person would know.

3

Never Pay With Gift Cards

No legitimate business or government agency accepts gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency for payments. These are red flags for scams.

4

Protect Personal Information

Never share your Social Security number, bank account details, or passwords over the phone, email, or text unless YOU initiated the contact.

5

Talk to Someone

Before sending money or sharing information, talk to a family member, friend, or trusted advisor. Scammers often isolate their victims.

6

Trust Your Instincts

If something feels wrong or too good to be true, it probably is. It's okay to hang up, delete the email, or say no.

Helpful Resources

Organizations that can help you stay safe

Report Fraud & Get Help

  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC): ReportFraud.ftc.gov or call 1-877-FTC-HELP
  • FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center: IC3.gov
  • AARP Fraud Watch Network: AARP.org/fraudwatchnetwork or call 1-877-908-3360
  • Social Security Fraud Hotline: 1-800-269-0271
  • IRS Impersonation Scam Reporting: Call 1-800-366-4484

Financial Protection

  • Contact your bank immediately if you've shared account information or sent money
  • Place a fraud alert on your credit reports by contacting one of the three credit bureaus
  • Consider a credit freeze to prevent new accounts from being opened in your name
  • Review your bank and credit card statements regularly for unauthorized charges
